# Research Specification: Hope or Hype — Can AI Boost Human Capital in the Global South?

**Date:** 2026-03-20
**Author:** Gabriel Demombynes (World Bank)

---

## Paper Type

Policy-oriented evidence review (not an original empirical paper). Audience: development policymakers and practitioners.

---

## Core Question

What does the rigorous evidence say about AI's potential to improve education and health outcomes in low- and middle-income countries, and under what conditions is that potential likely to be realized?

---

## Argument Structure

The paper is **agnostic in tone** — a balanced review, not an advocacy piece. It organizes evidence around two themes:

1. **Promise:** What causal studies show AI can do for education and health in LMICs
2. **Peril:** Risks and failure modes specific to AI in low-resource settings

A unifying **enabling factors framework** runs throughout: electricity, internet connectivity, devices, digital expertise, and institutions. This framework gives policymakers a practical lens — not "is AI good?" but "does our context have what AI requires?"

---

## Conceptual Framework

Two modalities through which AI can affect human capital:

| Modality | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct provision** | AI substitutes for the human service provider | AI tutors, AI therapists, direct-to-patient medical advice |
| **Augmentation** | AI assists frontline workers (humans in the loop) | Clinical decision support, teacher-facing tools, diagnostic aids |

The paper does not argue one modality is superior — it reviews evidence on both.

---

## Scope

- **Domains:** Education and health (livelihoods/social protection mentioned but peripheral)
- **Geography:** Low- and middle-income countries (Global South)
- **Evidence standard:** Focus on RCTs and quasi-experimental designs with causal identification; working papers and preprints included
- **AI types:** GenAI primary focus for education; broader AI (imaging, CDSS, surveillance) for health

---

## Contribution

Four differentiating features vs. existing reviews:

1. **Causal evidence filter** — focuses on RCTs and quasi-experiments; excludes observational-only studies
2. **Enabling factors framework** — structured analytical lens not applied in prior reviews
3. **Cross-domain scope** — combines education and health (most reviews are domain-specific)
4. **Policy orientation** — aimed at practitioners, not researchers; leads with implications

## Key Evidence (as of draft date)

**Education:**
- Choi et al. (2024) — Teacher.AI, Sierra Leone; descriptive; WhatsApp-based
- Henkel et al. (2024) — Rori chatbot, Ghana; RCT; 0.36 SD math gains
- De Simone et al. (2025) — Microsoft Copilot, Nigeria; RCT; 0.23 SD English gains

**Health:**
- Korom et al. (2025) — AI Consult, Kenya (Penda Health); 16% fewer diagnostic errors
- PROSPECT trial, Malawi — CAD4TB; TB detection RCT
- Multiple imaging/diagnostics studies (observational)

**Evidence gaps:** Mental health, livelihoods, long-term effects, cost-effectiveness
